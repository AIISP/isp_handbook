## §1 Theory

### 1.1 BLC — Black Level Correction

#### 1.1.1 Why does a dark frame have non-zero output?

An ideal pixel with zero incident photons should produce ADC code 0. In practice, three physical mechanisms prevent this:

**1. Dark current (thermal generation)**

Electron-hole pairs are generated by thermal excitation in the depletion region even without illumination. The dark current density $J_\text{dark}$ follows the Arrhenius relation, approximately doubling for every $\Delta T_2$ degrees Celsius of temperature rise:

$$J_\text{dark}(T_2) \approx J_\text{dark}(T_1) \cdot 2^{(T_2 - T_1)/\Delta T_2}$$

The doubling temperature $\Delta T_2$ is process-dependent; **the engineering range is 5–10 °C** (see Janesick 2007, Chapter 5; EMVA 1288 §3.6). Using $\Delta T_2 = 7\ ^\circ\text{C}$ as an example, a sensor heating from 25 °C to 60 °C accumulates approximately 32× the dark current.

> **Engineering note:** For typical smartphone still-capture exposure times (< 1/30 s), dark current contributes far less to total black level than the amplifier fixed offset. However, in video recording and long-exposure night modes, dark current temperature drift becomes the dominant error source and cannot be neglected.

**2. Fixed-pattern offset (FPN)**

Column-level and pixel-level differential amplifiers carry non-zero input-referred offsets that are highly reproducible frame-to-frame and manifest as spatial column streaks or block patterns. FPN is a stable DC bias independent of exposure time.

**3. ADC pedestal**

Sensor designs intentionally add a digital offset to the ADC output to prevent digital coding overflow when analog noise drives the signal slightly below the analog zero reference. Typical pedestals: 64–256 LSB for 10-bit sensors; 256–1024 LSB for 12-bit sensors.

The sum of these three contributions is the **black level** (or **optical black level**). It is the first DC bias that must be removed before any downstream stage can interpret digital codes as photon counts.

#### 1.1.2 Optical Black Region

Most image sensors reserve a ring of physically light-shielded pixels at the array periphery — the **optical black (OB) region** — to deliver a per-frame black level estimate that tracks gain and temperature variations in real time. Because OB pixels receive no light, their output equals the current black level including dark-current variation.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│   OB rows (shielded, top, typically 8–32 rows)   │
├──────┬───────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ OB   │                                           │
│ cols │     Active imaging area                   │
│(shld)│                                           │
└──────┴───────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Robust OB estimation: Trimmed Mean**

A simple mean of the OB region is not robust enough for engineering use. Key failure modes:

- OB rows adjacent to the active area can be contaminated by **light leakage**, biasing the mean high
- **Hot pixels** in the OB region systematically elevate the estimate
- On PDAF (phase detection AF) sensors, some OB pixels are partially open for AF purposes and behave differently from standard OB pixels

Recommended: use a **trimmed mean** per channel $c \in \{R, G_r, G_b, B\}$:

$$\text{OB}_c = \frac{1}{|P_c'|} \sum_{(i,j) \in P_c'} R(i, j)$$

where $P_c'$ is obtained by removing the bottom $\alpha\%$ and top $\beta\%$ quantiles of $P_c$. Typical values: $\alpha = 5$, $\beta = 10$ (the upper trim is larger to preferentially reject hot pixels and light leakage).

For sensors with significant row-level FPN, refine further with **row-wise OB estimation**:

$$\text{OB}_{c,i} = \text{trimmed\_mean}\bigl\{R(i,j) : (i,j) \in P_{c,i}\bigr\}$$

Then apply temporal smoothing (moving average or low-pass filter) across row estimates before using them for per-row correction.

#### 1.1.3 BLC Formula

The core BLC operation is **bias subtraction followed by clipping**. Normalization to $[0,1]$ is a separate, optional step. These two operations must not be merged into a single expression.

**Step 1: Offset subtraction (mandatory)**

$$R'_c(i,j) = \text{clip}\bigl(R_c(i,j) - B_c,\ 0,\ W_c\bigr)$$

where:
- $R_c(i,j)$: raw ADC code for channel $c$ at pixel $(i,j)$
- $B_c$: per-channel black level (from live OB estimation or calibration LUT)
- $W_c$: per-channel white level (full-well saturation code, e.g., 4095 for a 12-bit sensor)
- $\text{clip}(\cdot, 0, W_c)$: clamp to $[0, W_c]$ to prevent negative values from underflowing in integer arithmetic

The output domain is $[0, W_c]$ in integer ADC codes. ISP hardware pipelines typically pass data in this format; normalization is deferred to a later stage.

**Step 2: Normalization (optional, context-dependent)**

When downstream stages require a linear floating-point input in $[0, 1]$:

$$I_c^\text{BLC}(i,j) = \frac{R'_c(i,j)}{W_c - B_c}$$

> **Why two separate steps?** The combined form $(R_c - B_c) / (W_c - B_c)$ is **not equivalent** to clip-then-divide. Without the clip in Step 1, pixels below black level produce negative fractional values, not zero. Many ISP hardware blocks implement only Step 1 (integer bias subtraction); normalization is handled in the AWB or CCM stage.

**Per-channel correction is mandatory.** Even on the same die, the four Bayer channels' amplifier offsets can differ by 2–8 LSB. Using a single scalar black level for all channels introduces channel imbalance that propagates as a color cast through the entire pipeline.

#### 1.1.4 BLC LUT Indexing Dimensions

Black level varies across multiple physical parameters; the calibration LUT must cover all significant dimensions. Priority table:

| Index Dimension | Physical Cause | Priority |
|-----------------|---------------|----------|
| **Analog gain** | Amplifier offset scales with gain; different gain steps use different amp stages, each with its own offset | Mandatory |
| **HCG / LCG conversion gain mode** | High/Low conversion gain configurations use fundamentally different diff-pair settings; BL difference can reach tens of LSB | Mandatory if sensor supports it |
| **HDR exposure path (LEF / SEF)** | Multi-frame HDR reads each exposure through an independent readout path with different gain | Mandatory in HDR mode |
| **Sensor operating mode** | Full-res / 4-in-1 binning / PDAF crop affect pixel summation logic | Important |
| **Temperature** | Dark current scales with temperature; significant in long-exposure scenarios | Optional when OB region is available |
| **Exposure time** | Long-exposure dark current accumulation: $B_c(T_\text{exp}) = B_{c,0} + J_c \cdot T_\text{exp}$ | Mandatory for long-exposure |

Minimum recommended LUT structure:

```
blc_lut[analog_gain_idx][cg_mode][channel]  →  B_c  (integer, same bit-width as ADC)
```

When a live OB region is available, the LUT serves as initialization and a temperature-drift fallback. Without an OB region, the LUT is the sole black-level source and must cover temperature and exposure dimensions more thoroughly.

#### 1.1.5 Error Propagation Through the Signal Chain

A black-level error $\varepsilon_c = B_c^\text{used} - B_c^\text{true}$ propagates as an additive bias through every downstream stage:

- **AWB:** White balance gains are computed from channel ratios. If $\varepsilon_R \neq \varepsilon_B$, the apparent illuminant color shifts. A 4 LSB inter-channel OB error in a 10-bit system produces a visible color cast at ISO 3200.
- **CCM:** The color correction matrix assumes linear inputs. An additive black-level error pushes the operating point off the linear regime, elevating $\Delta E$.
- **Gamma / tone mapping:** Black point shifts; overcorrection hard-clips shadow detail; undercorrection lifts the noise floor in dark regions.

### 1.2 PDPC — Pixel Defect Detection & Correction

#### 1.2.1 Defect Taxonomy

Pixel defects arise from manufacturing process variation, thermal stress, and radiation damage. A critical engineering distinction is between **static defects** and **dynamic defects**:

**Static defects**
- Reliably detectable at factory test; stable across operating conditions
- Stored in sensor OTP (one-time programmable memory) or ISP NVM as a **factory defect map**
- Runtime correction: table lookup only — zero compute overhead

**Dynamic defects**
- Appear only under specific conditions: high-temperature hot pixels (temperature-dependent), high-gain RTS (random telegraph signal) noise pixels, new hot pixels accumulating from radiation
- Cannot be fully pre-characterized; must be detected on a per-frame basis at runtime

EMVA 1288 Release 4.0 (2021) defines the standard defect taxonomy:

| Defect Type | Behavior | Physical Cause |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| **Hot pixel** | Consistently elevated output regardless of illumination; approaches saturation in dark | Crystal defects causing excess dark current; exacerbated at high temperature or long exposure |
| **Dead pixel** | Persistently dark, near black level | Broken photodiode or column line |
| **Stuck pixel** | Fixed at an intermediate value, unresponsive to scene brightness | Gate oxide damage; radiation-induced charge traps |
| **Blinker** | Alternates between normal and defective states across frames | Partially filled charge traps; RTS noise |
| **Cluster defect** | ≥2 adjacent defective pixels | Neighboring defect spread or lithography defects |

#### 1.2.2 PDAF Pixels — Not Defects

On PDAF (phase detection AF) sensors, AF pixels achieve phase-difference sensing by physically blocking part of the microlens aperture. These pixels respond to light differently from neighbors and appear as systematic dark patterns in the RAW image.

**Misidentifying PDAF pixels as dead pixels is a common pipeline error with serious consequences:**

- PDAF pixel coordinates are fixed and predictable; they are stored in a separate firmware table (distinct from the defect map)
- Standard PDPC interpolation of a PDAF pixel destroys the phase information needed by the AF engine
- Qualcomm Spectra ISP has a dedicated PDAF Replacement path; libcamera/RPi handles PDAF pixels in the `rpi.af` module, fully separate from the PDPC path
- PDAF pixels should be processed by a dedicated PDAF correction module after BLC and before PDPC

#### 1.2.3 Detection Methods

**Method 1: Dark-frame method (static detection, calibration-time)**

Cover the lens; capture 64+ dark frames at target gain and temperature. Compute per-pixel mean $\bar{D}(i,j)$ and global statistics:

$$\text{score}_\text{hot}(i,j) = \frac{\bar{D}(i,j) - \bar{D}_\text{global}}{\sigma_{D,\text{global}}}$$

Flag as hot pixel if $\text{score} > \tau_\text{hot}$ (typical: 5–10). Dead pixels are detected analogously using flat-field frames.

**Method 2: Local neighborhood comparison (runtime dynamic detection)**

For each pixel $(i,j)$, sample same-channel neighbors within a $k \times k$ window in the Bayer grid (stride 2 to skip cross-channel pixels). **A signal-adaptive threshold is essential** — a fixed $\tau = 3.0$ works only for a uniform flat field. In bright regions (large shot noise) and dark regions (readout-noise dominated), the optimal threshold differs dramatically.

Signal-adaptive detection threshold (see Nakamura 2006, Chapter 4; Liu et al. 2006):

$$T(i,j) = \tau \cdot \sqrt{\sigma_r^2 + k_\text{gain} \cdot \hat{I}(i,j)}$$

where:
- $\sigma_r^2$: readout noise variance (fixed, from calibration)
- $k_\text{gain}$: shot noise coefficient at current analog gain ($\approx$ electrons per ADC code)
- $\hat{I}(i,j)$: local neighborhood mean (proxy for expected signal)
- $\tau$: sensitivity control coefficient, typical range 2.5–5.0 (tuned per miss-rate/false-alarm-rate target)

Detection condition:

$$|I(i,j) - \hat{I}(i,j)| > T(i,j)$$

**Static + dynamic combination strategy in practice:**

| Strategy | Latency | Coverage | Compute |
|----------|---------|----------|---------|
| Static map only (OTP/NVM lookup) | Zero | Factory-characterized stable defects | Minimal |
| Dynamic detection only | 1 frame | Full coverage including temperature-dependent hot pixels | Moderate |
| Hybrid (static + dynamic) | Zero + 1 frame | Optimal | Low (high static hit rate; dynamic only catches new defects) |

Engineering best practice: maintain a static defect map (updated via OTA or periodic recalibration), while running dynamic detection at a conservative threshold to catch newly appearing hot pixels at high temperature or high gain.

#### 1.2.4 Correction Methods

Once detected, a defective pixel's value is replaced by an estimate from its neighborhood. All corrections operate in the Bayer domain using **same-channel neighbors only** (Bayer stride 2), preserving the Bayer pattern intact for downstream demosaicing.

**Direction-adaptive interpolation**

Simple mean or median replacement introduces correction artifacts at edges. Direction-adaptive correction computes gradient-weighted estimates from each spatial direction:

$$I_\text{corr}(i,j) = \sum_{d \in \{H, V, D_1, D_2\}} w_d \cdot \hat{I}_d(i,j), \qquad w_d \propto \frac{1}{g_d + \epsilon}$$

where $g_d$ is the gradient magnitude along direction $d$ and $\epsilon$ prevents division by zero. Smaller gradient → flatter direction → higher weight → more reliable interpolation.

For an isolated single-pixel defect (Bayer stride 2):

$$\hat{I}_H(i,j) = \frac{I(i,j-2) + I(i,j+2)}{2}, \quad g_H = |I(i,j-2) - I(i,j+2)|$$
$$\hat{I}_V(i,j) = \frac{I(i-2,j) + I(i+2,j)}{2}, \quad g_V = |I(i-2,j) - I(i+2,j)|$$

**Median correction**

Suitable for cluster defects or fast approximations; neighborhood should be expanded to $5 \times 5$ or larger:

$$I_\text{corr}(i,j) = \text{median}\bigl\{I(r,c) : (r,c) \in \mathcal{N}(i,j),\ (r,c) \notin \text{defect\_map}\bigr\}$$

### 1.3 HDR Multi-Path BLC/PDPC

In staggered HDR (e.g., dual-exposure eSHDR), the long-exposure frame (LEF) and short-exposure frame (SEF) are read out through independent analog paths with different gain settings and different dark-current integration times. **BLC and PDPC must be applied independently to each exposure path** to preserve the linearity required by the downstream HDR merge.

A common mistake is applying the same black level to both LEF and SEF. Because the two frames use different gains, a shared black level introduces a systematic color error in the merged highlights that cannot be corrected by AWB after the merge.

## §2 Platform Implementation

### 2.1 Qualcomm Spectra ISP

Qualcomm Spectra series ISP (Spectra 480/580/680, corresponding to Snapdragon 865/888/8 Gen 1) implements black level and defect correction as separate hardware blocks:

- **OBC (Optical Black Correction):** Reads the OB region in real time and computes a per-channel trimmed mean; supports per-row OB estimation to compensate row-level FPN. Key registers: `black_level_lock`, `black_level_offset[4]` (four channels)
- **SDPC (Static Defect Pixel Correction):** Loads factory defect coordinates from EEPROM (up to 2048 entries); hardware LUT lookup with zero pipeline latency
- **DDPC (Dynamic Defect Pixel Correction):** Detects hot/dead pixels in real time; threshold registers `Fmin_threshold`, `Fmax_threshold`; supports direction-adaptive interpolation

Tuning toolchain: Qualcomm Snapdragon Profiler; Chromatix XML tuning files (`chromatix_bpcbcc_v20.xml`, `chromatix_abf_v34.xml`).

### 2.2 MediaTek Imagiq ISP

MediaTek Imagiq 5.0+ (Dimensity 9000/9300) processes black level correction in two pipeline stages:

- **P1 path (Pre-pipe, RAW domain):** BLC is completed in P1; computes real-time per-channel trimmed mean from OB region, with optional row-wise correction. Parameters stored in MTK private `tuning_para.json` under the `AWB_OB` section
- **P2 path (Color processing):** PDPC executes early in P2, with two tiers: static coordinate lookup + dynamic threshold detection. Supports independent PDAF pixel bypass path
- Tuning tool: MSDK (Mobile Solutions Development Kit) for BLC and PDPC parameter tables

### 2.3 HiSilicon Kirin ISP

- **2D Black Level Map:** Kirin 990/9000 ISP supports a two-dimensional BLC map with independent correction coefficients in both row and column directions, accommodating large-sensor designs with significant row/column FPN
- **Defect correction:** Static defect coordinates loaded from EEPROM; dynamic detection thresholds configurable per gain segment in the ISP tuning tool
- Android HAL3 interface: `android.sensor.blackLevelPattern` (4-element array for RGGB channels)

### 2.4 libcamera / Raspberry Pi PiSP

**Architecture note:** libcamera is an open-source camera framework — it does **not** contain a generic software BLC or PDPC implementation. It defines an IPA (Image Processing Algorithm) plugin interface; the actual algorithms are provided by platform-specific IPA modules.

For Raspberry Pi, the hardware ISP is the **PiSP** block on BCM2712 (Pi 5) or the Broadcom VC4 GPU ISP (Pi 4 and earlier). BLC parameters are configured through a JSON Tuning File:

```json
// File: /usr/share/libcamera/ipa/rpi/vc4/imx477.json
// (Raspberry Pi HQ Camera, Sony IMX477 sensor)
{
    "rpi.black_level": {
        "black_level": 4096   // 12-bit sensor; 4096/65535 ≈ 256 in 10-bit equivalent
    }
}
```

The libcamera RPi IPA `rpi.black_level` module is defined in `src/ipa/rpi/vc4/data/`; the C++ implementation is at `src/ipa/rpi/controller/rpi/black_level.cpp`. It reads OB statistics from the hardware ISP statistics block — no software per-row loop required.

PDPC is handled by the `rpi.dpc` module, controlled by a single `strength` parameter (0.0–1.0):

```json
{
    "rpi.dpc": {
        "strength": 1.0
    }
}
```

> **picamera2 users:** When loading a tuning file via `Picamera2(tuning=load_tuning_file("imx477.json"))`, BLC and PDPC parameters are consumed by the libcamera hardware IPA driver — the actual processing runs on the GPU/hardware ISP. The Python layer only passes the configuration; no BLC or PDPC logic executes in Python.

## §3 Calibration

### 3.1 BLC Calibration

**Calibration procedure:**

1. Mount sensor in a light-tight enclosure (lens fully capped); eliminate all stray light
2. Capture 32–64 dark frames per analog gain step; minimum gain coverage: ×1, ×2, ×4, ×8, ×16, ×64
3. For each frame, compute per-channel trimmed mean over the OB region; average across frames to suppress temporal noise
4. If the sensor supports HCG/LCG dual conversion gain, calibrate both modes independently and store separate LUTs
5. For multi-exposure HDR, calibrate LEF and SEF paths separately
6. Fit a piecewise-linear interpolation model across gain steps for runtime interpolation at non-standard gain values

```python
# Minimal BLC LUT structure (pseudocode)
blc_lut = {
    # key: (analog_gain_idx, cg_mode)   cg_mode: 0=LCG, 1=HCG
    # value: [B_R, B_Gr, B_Gb, B_B]    units: ADC LSB
    (0, 0): [256, 258, 257, 260],   # ×1 gain, LCG
    (0, 1): [312, 315, 314, 318],   # ×1 gain, HCG
    (1, 0): [264, 266, 265, 268],   # ×2 gain, LCG
    # ...
}
```

**EMVA 1288 compliance:** EMVA 1288 v4.0 (§3, §4) requires reporting Mean Dark Signal (MDS), Temporal Noise of Dark Signal, and Dark Signal Non-Uniformity (DSNU) separately.

### 3.2 PDPC Calibration

**Hot pixel detection (dark-frame method):**

1. Cover lens; set exposure to the maximum supported value at each gain step (maximizes hot pixel signal)
2. Capture 64 dark frames per gain step
3. Compute per-pixel mean $\bar{D}(i,j)$; if $\bar{D}(i,j) > \bar{D}_\text{global} + \tau_\text{hot} \cdot \sigma_{D,\text{global}}$, flag as hot pixel
4. Repeat at elevated temperature (e.g., 60 °C) to capture temperature-dependent hot pixels; store `gain_min` field to record the minimum gain level at which each defect is significant

**Dead pixel detection (flat-field method):**

1. Use an integrating sphere or uniform diffuse light source; set exposure to 70–80% saturation
2. If a pixel's response is below $\bar{F}_\text{global} - \tau_\text{dead} \cdot \sigma_{F,\text{global}}$, flag as dead pixel

**Defect map format:**

```python
defect_map = [
    {"row": 120, "col": 345, "type": "hot",   "gain_min": 1},   # present at all gains
    {"row": 512, "col": 789, "type": "hot",   "gain_min": 16},  # temperature-dependent hot pixel
    {"row": 233, "col": 410, "type": "dead",  "gain_min": 1},
    {"row": 311, "col": 522, "type": "stuck", "gain_min": 1},
]
```

## §4 Tuning

### 4.1 BLC Tuning

**Strategy for live OB vs. calibration LUT:**

- OB region available and clean (no light leakage, no PDAF contamination): prefer per-frame OB estimation; use calibration LUT only as initialization
- Mobile still capture with short exposure (< 1/30 s): calibration LUT typically sufficient; skip per-frame OB update
- Video recording / long-exposure night mode: per-frame OB estimation is mandatory to track temperature drift
- Sensors without an OB region: rely entirely on the calibration LUT; include temperature and exposure-time dimensions

**Long-exposure dark current compensation:**

For $T_\text{exp} > 1\ \text{s}$, compensate dark current accumulation with:

$$B_c(T_\text{exp}) = B_{c,0} + J_c \cdot T_\text{exp}$$

where $J_c$ (dark current rate, LSB/s) must be calibrated independently per analog gain and representative temperature.

### 4.2 PDPC Tuning

**Signal-adaptive threshold coefficient $\tau$:**

| $\tau$ | Typical behavior |
|--------|-----------------|
| $< 2.0$ | High false alarm rate; fine textures smeared; visible sharpness loss |
| 2.5–3.5 | General-purpose starting point; Precision/Recall trade-off must be verified |
| 4.0–5.0 | Conservative; only catches prominent defects; suitable for low-gain settings |
| $> 6.0$ | High miss rate for moderate hot pixels; visible white/black specks in uniform sky at high gain |

Tuning procedure: sweep $\tau$ from 1.5 to 6.0 on flat-field and textured images with a known ground-truth defect map; plot the Precision-Recall curve and select the $\tau$ at maximum $F_1$. Tune $\tau$ per gain segment — shot noise increases at high gain, requiring a higher $\tau$ to avoid false alarms.

**Neighborhood radius:**

- $3 \times 3$ (Bayer stride 2, radius 1): isolated single-pixel defects; hardware-friendly
- $5 \times 5$ (radius 2): stuck or blinking pixels, edge-adjacent cases
- $7 \times 7$ (radius 3): 2–3 pixel cluster defects

## §5 Artifacts

### 5.1 BLC Undercorrection

**Symptoms:** Residual DC bias lifts the black floor; shadow regions appear milky/hazy or show color tinting (if per-channel OB values are inaccurate).

**Root causes:**
- OB region contaminated by light leakage; trimmed mean is still biased high
- Calibration LUT out-of-date at elevated temperature (dark current increased but LUT not updated)
- Upper trim fraction $\beta$ too small; hot pixels not fully excluded

**Diagnosis:** Capture a dark frame through the complete pipeline; per-channel mean in the shadow region should be near 0. A positive residual > 1 LSB indicates undercorrection.

### 5.2 BLC Overcorrection

**Symptoms:** Shadow detail clipped to 0; near-black pixels map to hard black; shadow regions exhibit a flat, crushed appearance.

**Root causes:**
- OB mean estimate biased high (e.g., OB rows near the active area are partially lit and get excluded by the lower trim instead of retained)
- Calibration LUT contains conservatively (over-)estimated black level values

**Diagnosis:** Check the histogram of an 18% gray card image; excessive concentration at code 0 relative to expected Poisson width indicates overcorrection.

### 5.3 PDPC False Positives

**Symptoms:** Fine textures (hair, fabric) are smeared; shot noise in flat fields is "corrected away," artificially reducing apparent noise to below the theoretical Poisson floor.

**Root causes:** $\tau$ too low; or a fixed threshold used instead of signal-adaptive, resulting in high false alarm rates in bright regions where shot noise is large.

**Diagnosis:** Compare flat-field standard deviation before and after PDPC; overcorrection suppresses variance below $\sigma^2 < k_\text{gain} \cdot \bar{I}$.

### 5.4 PDPC False Negatives

**Symptoms:** Visible white or black specks in smooth regions (sky, skin, uniform walls).

**Root causes:** $\tau$ too high; or factory defect map incomplete (new hot pixels appearing at high temperature not yet recorded).

**Diagnosis:** Capture dark and flat-field frames; compare against the ground-truth defect map to compute miss rate.

### 5.5 BLC Temporal Drift (Video)

**Symptoms:** Progressive color cast during long video recordings, most pronounced during the first 3–8 minutes as the sensor warms up.

**Root causes:** Dark current increases with temperature, but OB statistics are not updated per frame (OB estimation disabled), causing the effective black level to be underestimated as the sensor heats up.

**Mitigation:** Enable per-frame OB region mean update. Alternatively, read the on-chip temperature sensor (`android.sensor.temperature` or platform-private interface) and apply Arrhenius compensation:

$$\hat{B}_c(T_\text{now}) = B_{c,\text{cal}} \cdot 2^{(T_\text{now} - T_\text{cal})/\Delta T_2}$$

$\Delta T_2$ must be measured during calibration for each sensor; do not assume a textbook value.

### 5.6 PDAF Pixels Misidentified as Defects

**Symptoms:** Regular bright/dark spots at AF pixel positions in the Bayer RAW; AF phase detection fails.

**Root causes:** PDPC does not distinguish the PDAF coordinate map from the defect map, treating AF masking pixels as dead pixels and replacing them with interpolated values.

**Prevention:** Exclude all PDAF pixel coordinates from the neighborhood computation in the PDPC module. PDAF pixels must be routed through a dedicated AF pixel correction module (see §1.2.2).

## §6 Evaluation

### 6.1 BLC Metrics

**Per-channel residual DC bias:**

After BLC, capture a dark frame through the full pipeline and compute:

$$\text{Residual}_c = \frac{1}{|P_{\text{active},c}|} \sum_{(i,j) \in P_{\text{active},c}} R'_c(i,j)$$

**Target:** $|\text{Residual}_c| < 0.5\ \text{LSB}$; cross-channel spread $\max_c - \min_c < 1.0\ \text{LSB}$.

### 6.2 PDPC Metrics

Using a calibrated ground-truth defect map:

$$\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}, \quad \text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}, \quad F_1 = \frac{2 \cdot P \cdot R}{P + R}$$

**Targets:** Recall > 99%, Precision > 95%, $F_1 > 0.97$.

Evaluate dynamic detection separately for low gain (×1–×4), medium gain (×8–×16), and high gain (×32+); each gain band typically requires independent threshold tuning.

### 6.3 End-to-End Quality Metrics

- **Gray card $\Delta E_{00}$:** Capture an 18% neutral gray card after BLC+PDPC; $\Delta E_{00} < 1.0$ relative to a D50 reference neutral
- **Flat-field uniformity:** Compute per-channel coefficient of variation (CV = $\sigma/\mu$) on an integrating-sphere flat field; target CV < 2%
- **EMVA 1288 DSNU:** Dark Signal Non-Uniformity; target $\text{DSNU}_{1288} < 0.5\%$ of full-well capacity
